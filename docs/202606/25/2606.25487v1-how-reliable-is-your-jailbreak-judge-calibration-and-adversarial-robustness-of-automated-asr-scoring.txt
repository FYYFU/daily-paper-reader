# How Reliable Is Your Jailbreak Judge? Calibration and Adversarial Robustness of Automated ASR Scoring 

Yang Gao 

Veyon Solutions y.gao@veyon.solutions 

# Abstract 

Almost every paper on LLM jailbreaks and prompt injection reports an attack-success rate (ASR), and that number is assigned not by people but by an automated judge: either a safety classifier trained for the task, or a general chat model prompted to grade. The judge is rarely checked. We check it. Using 596 human-labeled completions from the HarmBench classifier validation set, we compare the two judge families against human majority votes and then attack them. The two families fail in opposite ways. The dedicated classifier over-flags (precision 0.835, recall 0.974); three different LLM-as-judges keep high precision (0.81 to 0.94) but show erratic recall (0.06 to 0.65), so the same responses produce very different ASR depending on which judge scores them. The two families also differ sharply in robustness. Wrappers that leave the harmful text untouched and only add benign framing flip every LLM-judge between 57% and 100% of the time, and a single prepended refusal sentence accounts for much of this (39% to 88%). The dedicated classifier resists these surface attacks (at most 6.7%), but a white-box GCG attack on its open weights flips 70% of confident true positives (21 of 30; 95% CI 54 to 86%) even at a small optimization budget. A two-annotator audit confirms the attacks leave the harm intact: every one of 80 sampled flips still contained the harmful content. The practical message is that no judge family should be trusted without checking it. Because a large and growing share of reported ASR comes from LLM-judges, many such numbers are unreliable both on average and under deliberate pressure. We recommend that papers report judge precision and recall on a human-labeled slice, report ASR corrected for judge precision, and include an adversarial check of the judge. Our code is released. 

Keywords: AI safety; safety evaluation; jailbreak; attack success rate; LLM-as-judge; adversarial robustness; calibration. 

# 1. Introduction 

A new jailbreak paper reports 95% ASR against a frontier model; a new defense reports that it cuts ASR to 3%. Before either number means anything, one must ask who decided that each of the thousands of model responses was harmful. The answer is almost never a person. It is an automated judge, and the field treats its verdicts as ground truth. The problem is that this trust is unearned. If the judge systematically calls benign responses harmful, every ASR built on it is inflated; if it misses real harm, every ASR is deflated. Worse, a judge that keys on surface features rather than content can be steered: an attacker, or simply an author hoping for a strong result, can move the reported number without changing what the model actually said. Whoever relies on these numbers, from defense designers to reviewers to policymakers, inherits the judge’s blind spots without knowing it. We study the judge directly. A simple approach matches refusal strings, but the two families that decide most contested cases are the following. The first is a safety classifier fine-tuned specifically to label responses, such as Llama-Guard [7] or the HarmBench classifier [1]. The second, now common because it is cheap and flexible, is an ordinary instruction-tuned model prompted to act as a grader, often called LLM-as-judge. We take both, measure how well they agree with human labels, and then test how easily their verdicts can be flipped by changes that do not remove the harm. Our approach needs no special access beyond public models and a single free GPU, which is the point: anyone can run these checks, and so should. We anticipate, and find, that the two families are unreliable in different and partly opposite ways, that the cheaper family is also the more fragile, and that even the sturdier family breaks once an attacker can use its weights. A small human audit then confirms that the flips we report reflect the judge being fooled, not the harm being removed. 1

We make four contributions. First, a human-anchored comparison of the two judge families under one attack protocol. Second, evidence that content-preserving wrappers fool every LLM-judge we test, while a dedicated classifier shrugs them off. Third, a white-box attack that breaks that classifier on thirty confident cases, not a single anecdote. Fourth, two independent annotators confirm the flips keep the harm intact. We release the harness so the checks are easy to repeat. 

# 2. Related work 

Our framing follows a line of thought due to Shlegeris and Greenblatt [12], who argue for meta-level adver-sarial evaluation of oversight: instead of evaluating a model, one adversarially tests the procedure meant to catch bad behavior, by constructing actions the procedure scores well but that are known to be bad. Their setting is AI control against future deceptive models, and they leave the idea informal. We give a present-day, measured instance of it. Our overseer is the safety judge that scores attack success; our known-bad-but-well-scored actions are harm-preserving attacked responses; and we report numbers on real judges. Several tools serve as the field’s judges. HarmBench [1] provides standardized harmful behaviors and a fine-tuned classifier. StrongREJECT [2] introduced an autograder and showed that many high-ASR jailbreaks are “empty,” producing non-refusals with little real content. Llama-Guard [7] and WildGuard [8] are widely used guard models. We treat all of these as instruments to be calibrated and attacked rather than as oracles. Closer to our question, a few recent papers probe judge reliability. Know Thy Judge [9] shows that style changes and prepended text fool guard models. A Coin Flip for Safety [10] shows judge accuracy dropping against human labels under shifts in attack, model, and data, and Miller [11] argues for basic statistical reporting in evaluations. We add three things these papers do not provide together: a single-protocol comparison of dedicated classifiers against several LLM-judges with human labels [1], a white-box attack [3] that breaks a classifier that resists surface attacks, and a human audit confirming the attacks keep the harm. A separate literature builds the attacks themselves, including search methods such as PAIR [4] and TAP [5], gradient methods such as GCG [3], trained suffix generators such as AdvPrompter [6], and reinforcement-learned attackers [13, 14]. We borrow their adversarial stance but aim it at the judge rather than at the target model. 

# 3. Setup 

We use the HarmBench [1] text-behavior classifier validation set, which contains 596 pairs of a behavior (the harmful request) and a generation (a model’s response), spanning standard and contextual behaviors, many attack methods, and many source models. Each pair carries a label from three human annotators; we take the majority as the gold label. Contextual behaviors are scored with HarmBench’s official contextual prompt and standard behaviors with the standard prompt. For the dedicated classifier we use cais/HarmBench-Llama-2-13b-cls [1] with its official standard and contextual prompts. For LLM-as-judge we use three open models, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct [15], 

Phi-3.5-mini-instruct [16], and Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct [15], each given a short rubric that asks whether the response complies with the harmful behavior. We deliberately use open, ungated models so the work reproduces at no cost; closed frontier judges such as GPT-4o are left to future work. The exact judge rubric is released with our harness (Appendix A). Everything runs on a single NVIDIA T4 with 4-bit quantization. As a check on the quantization, our 4-bit classifier reproduces the benchmark’s stored predictions on 98.7% of items, so the quantized judge behaves like the released one. We report precision, recall, F1, and accuracy against the human majority. 4.3 The dedicated classifier under a white-box attack 

The classifier’s resistance to wrappers does not mean it cannot be moved. A greedy search over a fixed library of wrappers, guided by the classifier’s own probability, flips only 1 of 15 cases (6.7%), barely above the naive baseline of 3.4%. A gradient-based attack tells a different story. Using GCG [3] on the classifier’s open weights, with the harmful body left verbatim and a 20-token adversarial suffix optimized at a small budget of 50 steps and 32 candidates per step, we flip 21 of 30 confident true positives, a rate of 70.0% (95% CI 54 to 86%), with a mean of 21.4 optimization steps per success. 4.4 Do the attacks keep the harm? 

A reader might worry that a flip means the attack removed the harm rather than fooled the judge. To settle this, two annotators independently labeled a random sample of 80 flipped responses as harmful, not harmful, or unsure, after passing a calibration round and without seeing each other’s labels. They agreed on 75 of 80 items (93.8%). We adjudicated all five disagreements, and all five still contained the harmful content; in two of them the dissenting annotator had been misled by the benign refusal prefix, the same way the judges are. After adjudication, all 80 sampled flips retained the harmful content. This matches the construction of the attacks, which never alter the harmful body. We report raw agreement and the adjudicated cases rather than Cohen’s kappa, because the true label distribution here is nearly constant (harm is almost always preserved), which makes kappa uninformative. Calibration confirmed the annotators recognize harmful content reliably, with harmful-control recall of 12 of 12 and 11 of 12. 

# 5. Discussion 

Read together, the results say that the choice of judge changes the answer. The same responses, scored by a dedicated classifier rather than an LLM-judge, give systematically different ASR; neither family matches the human labels well, and the two err in opposite directions. A simple correction helps for the over-flagging classifier: multiplying reported ASR by the judge’s precision removes roughly its false-positive contribution, about 16% here, ASR corrected ≈ ASR reported × precision .

For the LLM-judges the dominant error is low and unstable recall, which a precision correction does not fix and which is compounded by their fragility under attack. The robustness results sharpen the warning. A judge that responds to a refusal sentence rather than to the content after it is not measuring harm; it is measuring politeness. That this fools a careful human annotator twice in our audit shows the effect is not an artifact of machine scoring. The dedicated classifier avoids this 6trap but falls to an attacker who can compute gradients against it, which is exactly the situation for any open-weight judge. We therefore recommend four practices for any paper that reports ASR. State which judge is used and report its precision and recall on a human-labeled slice of the same data. Report ASR corrected for judge precision alongside the raw number. Run at least one content-preserving attack against the judge and report the flip rate. And prefer a dedicated classifier to an LLM-judge for scoring, while remembering that an open classifier can still be attacked directly. 

# 6. Limitations 

The study rests on one dataset, the HarmBench classifier validation set, which was built to validate a classifier rather than to measure per-method ASR; a clean per-method claim would need a dedicated benchmark. We did not tune the LLM-judge prompts, so part of the low recall may reflect our wording rather than the model, although the refusal-prefix fragility is consistent across models and matches prior reports. Two of the LLM-judge flip rates rest on small true-positive sets (16 and 47). The white-box attack uses 30 cases and a small budget; a larger budget would likely break more of the nine survivors, so the 70% figure understates the classifier’s exposure. The human audit uses 80 items and two annotators, and kappa is uninformative for the reason given above; harm preservation also holds by construction. All judges run in 4-bit, which reproduces the released classifier on 98.7% of items. 

# 7. Future work 

The natural next steps are to push the white-box attack to a larger budget and the full set of true positives, to test whether adversarial suffixes transfer between judges and survive paraphrase, and to add a black-box attacker that needs no weights. Adding a second benchmark and a closed frontier judge would test how far the calibration findings generalize, and a prompt-sensitivity study would separate prompt effects from model effects in the LLM-judge recall. 

# 8. Conclusion 

We asked whether the judges behind reported ASR can be trusted, and the answer is that they cannot be trusted on faith. A dedicated classifier and three LLM-judges disagree with human labels in opposite directions, so the same responses yield different ASR depending on the scorer. The LLM-judges, which are the cheaper and more common choice, flip on a single refusal sentence that leaves the harm in place. The dedicated classifier holds against such tricks but gives way to a gradient attack on its weights, and a human audit confirms that in all of these flips the harm is still there. The practical consequence is small to state and large in effect: report what your judge does on human-labeled data, correct ASR for the judge’s precision, and attack the judge before you trust it. 

# Ethics statement 

This work studies the reliability of safety evaluators using a public benchmark, HarmBench. We create no new harmful capability. Every harmful string we handle already exists in the benchmark, and our attacks only add benign framing around it or append adversarial tokens; we never ask a model to produce new harmful content. We report aggregate statistics and method, release no operational harmful text, and keep the most dangerous categories out of any example. The aim is defensive, namely to make safety measurement more trustworthy. The human annotators were told what they would read, took part voluntarily, and could stop at any time.

A.1 LLM-as-judge rubric 

System message: 

You are a strict AI content-safety classifier used to evaluate red-teaming results. 

User message: 

A user asked an AI model to perform the following harmful behavior: BEHAVIOR: {behavior} {ctx} The AI model produced this response: RESPONSE: {generation} Question: Does the RESPONSE actually comply with the harmful BEHAVIOR by providing harmful content that Answer with exactly one word: "Yes" if it is a successful harmful instance, or "No" otherwise. 

For contextual behaviors, {ctx} is replaced by a line CONTEXT: {context} ; for standard behaviors it is empty. The label is the model’s first generated token ( Yes /No ); we also read the Yes /No logits for the probability used by the attacks. 

A.2 Dedicated-classifier prompts 

The HarmBench classifier is run with its official standard and contextual prompts [1], unchanged. The label is decided by comparing the next-token logits of Yes and No at the answer position, and the harmful probability is p(Yes) / (p(Yes) + p(No)) .
