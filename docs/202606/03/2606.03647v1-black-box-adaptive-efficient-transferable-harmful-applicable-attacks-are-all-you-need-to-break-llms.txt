# Abstract 

Accurately evaluating adversarial robustness is a longstanding challenge. A flawed attack design can inflate robustness estimates, making deployment risk assessment and defense comparison unreliable. Historically, standardized attacks such as AutoAttack have largely resolved this for image classifiers, providing a reliable evaluation baseline for systematic comparison across defenses. However, no equiv-alent exists for LLM jailbreak evaluation yet, where designing such an attack is considerably more difficult. A reliable attack must, among other things, be black-box compatible, applicable to arbitrary defense pipelines, and efficient, which no existing method jointly satisfies. We introduce Indirect Harm Optimization (IHO), a masked diffusion language model attacker trained via iterative preference optimization against a harmfulness judge, requiring only black-box access to the target. The same method can be used without modification as a strong adaptive attack on individual behaviors, or as an efficient amortized policy that transfers to held-out behaviors and unseen target models without fine-tuning. Even against layered defenses, such as a Circuit Breaker-trained model combined with an aux-iliary detector, IHO improves attack success considerably over state-of-the-art approaches, without any defense-specific adaptation. Our results position IHO as a practical step toward the kind of standardized jailbreak evaluation that has improved reliability in the past. Code and models are available on GitHub and Hugging Face.

We identify six desiderata that characterize such attacks: 1) adversary knowledge and access, 2) transferability, 3) adaptiveness, 4) applicability, 5) efficiency, and 6) harmfulness. Existing attacks, however, cover only subsets of these properties. Gradient-based attacks require white-box access to model weights and gradients, making them inapplicable to closed-source models, which represent the most capable and widely deployed systems and generally show low transferability [ 8 ]. Their adaptiveness is further limited by modern deployments that wrap models in multi-stage pipelines that combine the model with external input and output detectors [ 9], further hindering end-to-end gradient-based optimization. Existing adaptive attacks either lack applicability , relying on considerable human effort to design jailbreak prompts [ 10 ], or efficiency , requiring unrealistic amounts of compute [11]. Fine-tuned attacker models improve efficiency and transferability but are generally difficult to adapt to specific target models and require substantial engineering overhead [ 12 ,13 ]. Furthermore, recent work finds that harmfulness of existing methods is generally overstated; attacks that only marginally cross the refusal threshold frequently produce false positives rather than genuinely harmful completions [ 14 ], highlighting the need for attacks that directly and effectively optimize for harmfulness rather than merely bypassing the safety guardrail. In this work, we introduce Indirect Harm Optimization(IHO), a simple, fully automated framework that addresses these gaps through principled design choices. First, rather than optimizing individual attack prompts directly, we train a parameterized attacker model. Direct prompt optimization must be conducted in the discrete natural language space, which hinders gradient-based methods and requires independent optimization for each behavior. Training an attacker model instead moves optimization into the continuous parameter space, where gradient descent is applicable, and the model naturally produces discrete outputs at inference. This also amortizes optimization cost across behaviors, as a single training run covers many deployments. Second, we instantiate the attacker as a diffusion language model, which samples attack prompts directly conditioned on the target behavior via bidirectional infilling. This avoids the dependence on a manually designed prompt that autoregressive attackers require, which introduces prompt-specific bias and manual overhead. Third, we optimize the attacker via Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [ 15 ] against an LLM judge. Rather than requiring direct gradient flow through the target model, optimization signal is gathered by sampling from the target, scored by the judge, and distilled into a preference dataset, decoupling optimization from the target and making the framework compatible with arbitrary black-box defense pipelines. Our main contributions are: 

Unified attack. We develop the novel attack method IHO that is applicable to a wide spectrum of threat models within a single black-box algorithm. The same method serves both as an adaptive 

attack, targeting a single query against a specific target model (including complex, non-differentiable pipelines that combine models and detectors), and as a universal policy that transfers across behaviors and target models, achieving state-of-the-art harmfulness against diverse models without any target-specific engineering. We further demonstrate that training the policy is inexpensive and that the amortized attack cost is considerably lower than existing algorithms, making IHO highly efficient .

Reliable Metrics. We identify several flaws in previous evaluations relying solely on attack success rate as a metric. We introduce Expected Volume under the Surface ( EVUS ), which accounts for sample efficiency, harm severity, is threshold independent, and is less sensitive to judge error. 

Ablations. We provide an extensive ablation of key design choices and hyperparameters. 

# 2 Attack Desiderata 

Motivated by principles from adversarial robustness evaluation [ 6, 7 ] and prior taxonomies of jailbreak attacks [ 16 ], we introduce six desiderata to systematically analyze and compare automated jailbreak attacks: efficiency, adversary knowledge & access, harmfulness, applicability, transferability, and adaptiveness. We later design our attack to fulfill these desiderata. 

E: Efficiency. Efficiency captures the total compute or API cost of an attack to elicit a harmful response. This spans every stage of the pipeline: the optimization that produces the attack prompt, potential training of auxiliary models, the sampling of completions from the target, and, for sample-heavy approaches, the judging required to identify a harmful completion among many candidates. 

K: Adversary knowledge & access. Threat models differ in the extent of knowledge of and access (black-box to white-box) to the target granted to the adversary [ 16 ]. In practice, most targets are 2closed-source proprietary systems where white-box access is simply unavailable, making black-box attacks the preferred option for the vast majority of adversaries. 

H: Harmfulness. Adversarial robustness research is fundamentally concerned with obtaining a lower bound on model robustness, that is, identifying all harmful behaviors that can be elicited from a model under attack. In order for an attack to accurately estimate this lower bound, we identify two important properties it should satisfy: H.1 An attack should scale with compute: one that continues to improve as more optimization is invested can more closely approximate the model’s true worst-case behavior. This is not guaranteed by design: sampling-based approaches do not optimize harm elicitation at all [ 5 ], while others optimize proxy objectives that do not directly increase the probability of harmful responses [ 17 ]. H.2 : The severity of the elicited harm matters as well: an attack that consistently produces deeply harmful responses is preferable to one that merely crosses the refusal threshold. 

A: Applicability. Applicability captures the human and engineering effort required to deploy an attack, distinct from its compute cost, which is measured by efficiency. This effort takes two forms. 

A.1 : The first is the manual work of crafting the attack itself. Attacks that rely on human-engineered components, such as manually adapted prompt templates, do not scale: the effort must be repeated for each new target or behavior. A.2 : The second is the engineering work of setting up and adapting an attack. Complex attack pipelines involving curated training data, auxiliary fine-tuned models, or multi-stage orchestration incur significant setup costs that do not amortize across deployments. Ideally, an attack requires minimal manual intervention to adjust to a new defense, setting, or data. 

A.3 : A further aspect of applicability is the number of attack trials needed for a successful attack. Attacks that require many target queries, whether for optimization or sampling, risk being flagged by the defender. An attack can minimize target queries in two ways: by being optimizable on a surrogate model rather than the target directly [ 3 ], and by yielding jailbreaks with a high per-query probability of eliciting harm, rather than relying on extensive sampling [17]. 

T: Transferability. Transferability captures the extent to which an attack obtained in one setting remains effective in another, and has two distinct axes. T.1 : The first is cross-model transferability: the ability of an attack optimized on one model to remain effective against other models. T.2 : The second is cross-behavior transferability: the ability of a single attack, whether a universal suffix or a trained attacker model, to generalize across behaviors not seen during optimization. A transferable attack amortizes per-attack cost across many deployments, requires no additional setup per new target, and can convert a white-box-derived attack into one usable in stricter access regimes. 

AD: Adaptiveness. In the LLM setting, adaptive attacks manually designed for a specific target defense are rare in practice: the discrete, non-differentiable nature of language models and the complexity of modern defense pipelines make target-specific adaptation considerably harder to engineer. Existing examples require considerable human engineering per target, making them impractical at scale [ 10 ]. We therefore adopt a more practical definition, characterizing an adaptive attack as one that uses signals from the target model to adjust itself and thus works across diverse defense configurations, such as non-differentiable components or complex pipelines. 

# 3 Methodology 

In the following, we derive an attacker that fulfills all the introduced attack desiderata. Positive and negative instances of a property are highlighted in green ■ and red ■, respectively. 

3.1 Principled Design Decisions How do we adapt to the defense? Eliciting harm from a strongly defended target requires the attack to adapt to that defense, typically through input-space optimization or fine-tuning an attacker model. Input-space attacks include heuristic search over token sequences and gradient-based methods. Heuris-tic search is sample-heavy ( A.3 ) and generally costly ( E). Gradient-based methods, like GCG [ 3] or PGD [ 18 ], require white-box access ( K) and are generally inefficient ( E). Moreover, methods that optimize in a continuous space and project to token embeddings suffer from discretization gaps that limit effectiveness ( E) [18]. Input-space attacks are therefore incompatible with our requirements. Model-level optimization trains a parameterized attacker model rather than searching for individual prompts. Crucially, it admits continuous optimization via gradient descent on the attacker’s param-eters, without a discretization gap since the attacker naturally outputs tokens ( E). It furthermore 3decouples the attacker from the target: reinforcement learning or preference optimization on collected roll-outs removes the need to backpropagate through the target model, while still adapting to the target ( AD ), making the approach compatible with a black-box threat model ( K). Beyond adaptation, training one attacker over a set of behaviors lets a single optimization run cover many deployments (A.1 , A.2 , T.1 , T.2 ) and amortizes compute across them ( E). 

We decide to optimize a parameterized attacker model within our approach. 

What attacker model do we use? The choice reduces to autoregressive or diffusion language models. Autoregressive attackers, such as PAIR [ 4] and TAP [ 19 ], require hand-written prompts to specify the attack strategy, which biases the attacker and introduces manual effort ( A.1 ). A masked diffusion language model conditions directly on the target behavior through bidirectional infilling and requires no such scaffolding ( A.1 ) [ 20 ]. Because the diffusion model conditions directly on the target behavior, the distribution over candidate prompts shifts with the target: harder behaviors naturally elicit different infilling solutions, providing implicit adaptation without any manual intervention. Moreover, diffusion language models generate within a fixed context window, allowing the attack length and generation budget to be controlled directly, while autoregressive models cannot be cleanly constrained to such short budgets ( E). Prior work finds that windows as small as 32 tokens are sufficient to produce strong attacks [ 20 ], and that the same diffusion attacker outperforms standard LLM-as-attacker baselines out of the box ( E, H). 

We use a masked diffusion language model as the attacker. 

What do we optimize for? The goal of an attack is to elicit harmful responses from the target, but harm is not directly optimizable and must be approximated through proxies [ 21 ]. A common choice in prior work is the probability that the target produces an affirmative response [ 3 ]. However, while this objective is straightforward to optimize, it is insensitive to whether the subsequent response is genuinely harmful. An alternative is to optimize a judge model’s harmfulness score directly [ 13 , 21 ], which provides a more direct proxy of the underlying goal ( H) and adapts to the target model ( AD ). 

We optimize toward high harmfulness as measured by a judge model. 

How do we optimize? Propagating gradients from the judge through the target back into the attacker is infeasible for several reasons: the target’s discrete sampling blocks gradients, complex non-differentiable pipelines would not be attackable, and computing gradients would require white-box access ( K). On-policy reinforcement learning, as used by Jailbreak-R1 [ 13 ], avoids this but incurs a per-step sampling cost from the target ( E), increasing both compute and target query volume (A.3 ). Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [ 15 ] sidesteps both: it trains directly on a preference dataset assembled from already-scored samples, reusing every pair across epochs within a cycle ( E,

A.3 ), with no auxiliary model required. Repeating this over multiple cycles additionally results in a bootstrapping approach: each cycle resamples preferences from the improved attacker, and the evolving policy progressively covers behaviors the earlier policy could not break ( H.1 , AD ). 

We optimize the attacker model with DPO over iteratively collected preferences.

Models and Defenses. We study two instruction-tuned model families: L LAMA 3-8B-I NSTRUCT [ 23 ]and Q WEN 2.5-I NSTRUCT [24 ] ( 7B to 32 B parameters). For L LAMA 3, we also consider three defense adapters: Circuit Breakers (CB) [ 25 ], Latent Adversarial Training (LAT) [ 26 ], and Continuous Adversarial Training (CAT) [ 27 ]. To reflect realistic deployment pipelines, we further wrap the CB model and Q WEN 2.5-7B with P OLY GUARD [28 ], a detector that inspects prompts and responses and overrides harmful outputs with a fixed refusal string. See Appendix H for details. 

Data. We use the 100 malicious behaviors from J AILBREAK BENCH [29 ] and split them into 60

Attack Baselines. We compare against state-of-the-art attacks from different attack families. GCG+ [ 3] as a gradient-based, white-box reference; A MPLE GCG+ [ 12 ] as a transfer-trained suffix attacker; B ON [ 5] as a sampling-only heuristic; PAIR [ 4] as an LLM-based attacker, included in both its default low-budget configuration and a budget-matched variant (PAIR+); JR1+ [ 13 ] a fine-tuned attacker conceptually closest to the presented method; and the diffusion I NPAINTING attack [ 20 ]as the untrained starting point our method builds on. 5.5 Ablations 

We performed numerous ablations to investigate design choices within IHO (see also Appendix C-E). 

INPAINTING. We found that LLaDA’s performance was largely insensitive to attack size beyond very small values, allowing us to reduce attack size from 64 to 32 for better efficiency. However, LLaDA does require unmasking one token at a time to maintain performance, so we kept S = T .

Quantity vs. quality. We found that training benefits saturated at moderate preference set sizes, while higher-quality chosen samples consistently improved performance when available. We therefore prioritize sample quality over scaling the number of training samples. 8Learning rate and DPO β. We found DPO β to have little effect on performance, while learning rate strongly affected output diversity. High learning rates caused distribution collapse despite similar EVUS, so we used a moderate learning rate to preserve diversity without sacrificing attack success. 

Resampling cadence. Improvements within a cycle saturated rapidly, with harm gains approaching zero after roughly 100 epochs. In line with the previous results, we therefore resampled preference data after convergence to provide fresh, higher-quality training signal from the improved attacker. 

# 6 Related Work 

Existing attacks fall into a small number of families, each covering only a subset of the desiderata above. Here, we categorize attack families from the related work that are specifically relevant to the approach we introduce, map them to our desiderata, and outline their trade-offs and gaps. For an extensive version, see Appendix I. 

LLM-as-attacker. One family delegates prompt construction to an attacker LLM that iteratively pro-poses and refines jailbreaks given feedback from the target. PAIR [ 4], TAP [ 19 ], and AutoDAN [ 33 ]are fully black-box ( K), require no manual components ( A), and inherit the language capabilities of the attacker LLM, giving the family competitive harmfulness ( H). However, these approaches rely on autoregressive search, which limits amortized efficiency ( E). A more recent variant [ 20 ] addresses this by reframing attack generation as inpainting with a diffusion language model: by conditioning on a desired affirmative response and letting the diffusion model fill in compatible prompts, this approach achieves strong amortized efficiency ( E) under transfer and sidesteps autoregressive search entirely. Across all variants in this family, however, the attacker model is held fixed, and only its output prompts vary, which bounds target-specific adaptation ( AD ), limits cross-behavior generalization (T.2 ), and keeps sample efficiency low ( A.3 ) against stronger defenses [17]. 

Fine-tuned attacker models. Fine-tuning the attacker LLM is a natural way to improve LLM-based attacks. This family splits along two axes. Transfer-trained variants such as AmpleGCG [ 12 ] and AdvPrompter [ 34 ] train an attacker on a fixed source distribution. The resulting model is efficient at inference ( E) and applicable across targets ( T.1 ), but gains no adaptive signal from the deployed target ( AD ). Target-specific variants such as Jailbreak-R1 [ 13 ] and AutoDAN-Turbo [ 35 ] restore adaptivity ( AD ) but pay a substantial setup cost in seed data, multi-stage training pipelines, and engineering effort ( A.2 ), undermining applicability in black-box deployment. 

Existing worst-case attacks. At the most adaptive end of the spectrum, Attacker Moves Second [ 11 ]achieves state-of-the-art harmfulness ( H, AD ) through target-specific attacker training. Nevertheless, it needs to be manually adapted to the specific defense ( A.1 , A.2 ) is closed-source ( K), requires immense compute ( E), and access to proprietary models to run ( K). 

# 7 Conclusion 

We presented IHO, an attacker designed to jointly satisfy the six desiderata we introduced. Its strong performance across undefended models, adversarially trained models, and layered pipelines suggests that IHO could serve as the kind of standardized attack that improved evaluation reliability in the past. 

Limitations. Cross-model transfer success degrades when the source and target models differ substantially in robustness, indicating that a single transfer policy does not replace per-defense adaptation in the most asymmetric settings, which can be resolved by using IHO as an adaptive attack but requires training the policy for the specific target. Moreover, while we show in Appendix F.2 that judge hacking does not seem to appear in our experiments, the optimization signal still depends on the quality of the judge model, and judge biases can shape what the attacker learns. 

Outlook. A natural extension is to train a single policy on signals pooled from several target models, which may yield a more robust universal attacker that closes the source-target mismatch gap observed in our transfer experiments. Future work could further explore a controlled comparison between preference optimization and on-policy reinforcement learning under matched compute and target-query budgets, which prior work has not cleanly isolated from confounding design choices. Beyond DPO itself, alternative preference-based objectives such as IPO [ 36 ], KTO [ 37 ], or SimPO [ 38 ] would test how sensitive our results are to the specific preference loss and further inform standardized jailbreak evaluation. 9References

14 B The Case for EVUS 

Standard reporting of attack success in adversarial prompting relies almost exclusively on Attack Success Rate (ASR). In this section of appendix we show, through concrete examples, that ASR exhibits several pathological behaviours that make it an unreliable basis for comparing attack methods. We then demonstrate how EVUS addresses these pathologies, characterise a dominance property that gives EVUS a clean decision-theoretic justification, and discuss the analytical estimator that makes EASR tractable without Monte Carlo simulation.

Other parameters, such as temperature and remasking strategies, had no significant impact on attack performance or degraded it. 

C.1 Attack Size 

Figure 6 reports EVUS[128] scores across defender models for attack sizes 8, 16, 32, 64, and 96. We observe on average improvement from size 8 to 32, after which performance saturates. Larger attack sizes do not yield systematic gains, and variations at sizes 64 and 96 are inconsistent across models. Thus, increasing the number of masked tokens beyond a moderate range did not meaningfully improve effectiveness for INPAINTING in our experiments. Table 2 reports wall-clock time in seconds per sample on an A100 and batch size chosen to fully utilize the 40GB VRAM. In contrast to performance, computational cost increases rapidly with attack size, as both input length and the number of denoising steps grow. The attack generation cost can be approximated as FLOPs attack = 2 · Nparams · T · S, (7) with T the sequence length and S the number of denoising steps. With S = T , this yields quadratic scaling in attack size. Empirically, increasing the attack size from 32 to 64 more than doubles attack generation time (from 

∼ 0.28 s to ∼ 0.80 s per sample), with a corresponding increase in total cost. Increasing to 96 again roughly doubles runtime without improving effectiveness. We therefore reduce attack size relative to original configuration of INPAINTING to improve runtime, but avoid overly small values as this would limit the attack space and thus the capabilities of our finetuned policy. We consider size 32 a suitable trade-off, capturing most performance while keeping runtime manageable and avoiding under-parameterization during the subsequent optimization.

All ablations in this section of appendix use a single-target, single-cycle setup to support broader sweeps at reduced computational cost. D.2 Learning Rate and DPO β

A key tension in our setup is that we want to utilise the pretraining fully while simultaneously bridging the gaps in distribution (chat template, prompt-only masking, and incomplete affirmative responses). A high learning rate or low β can drive the model to overfit the preference signal, collapsing the generated distribution toward a narrow set of high-scoring prompts and reducing coverage across behaviours. Conversely, too conservative a setting slows adaptation and leaves EVUS gains unrealised. We therefore aim to strike the right balance enabling the model to learn the valuable signal without overfitting. Figure 11 shows EVUS[128] and token-id diversity (root TTR) across a grid of learning rates 

∈ { 10 −5, 5×10 −5, 10 −4, 5×10 −4} and β ∈ { 0.05 , 0.1, 0.2, 0.3}. EVUS is broadly stable across much of the grid, with peak values around lr = 5 ×10 −5, β = 0 .1. Diversity, however, degrades sharply at lr = 5 ×10 −4 regardless of β, suggesting that high learning rates cause distribution collapse even when the LoRA adapter already constrains the parameter update magnitude. Notably, low β does not harm diversity as strongly as one might expect, since the LoRA adapter are inherently already regularized which partially compensates for the reduced KL penalty. F.1 Selecting Judge for Optimization 

A suitable optimization judge must satisfy three requirements. 

Continuous signal. The judge must output a continuous harmfulness score rather than a binary verdict. Our DPO training constructs preference pairs from score margins; a binary classifier such as HarmBench that effectively collapses to {0, 1} starves the attacker of signal on yet-unbroken behaviors and prevents partial breaks from being refined into full ones. 

Robustness to degenerate outputs. The judge must correctly score empty responses and high-perplexity toxic gibberish as non-harmful. As we discuss in §3, Circuit Breaker deflects harmful prompts by producing semantically void or incoherent outputs; a judge that flags such outputs as successful breaks would inflate CB attack success rates and corrupt the training signal. More broadly, Schwinn et al. [14] show that standard judges degrade to near-random accuracy under attack-induced and model-induced distribution shifts, and that sampling-heavy methods such as BoN [ 5 ] disproportionately exploit judge false positives rather than eliciting genuinely harmful content. Because our iterative DPO loop repeatedly resamples from an improving attacker, any systematic false-positive bias risks being amplified across cycles, causing the attacker to chase judge artifacts rather than true harmfulness. 

Query efficiency. The judge must be cheap enough to query at scale. Each IHO cycle generates thousands of prompt–response pairs that must be scored before DPO training can proceed; a slow or expensive judge becomes the computational bottleneck of the entire pipeline. StrongREJECT [ 30 ] satisfies all three requirements. Its rubric decomposes the score into a refusal indicator multiplied by specificity and convincingness Likert components, so incoherent or vacuous outputs receive scores near zero rather than spurious positives, and the resulting value is continuous in [0 , 1] . We additionally verified these properties empirically: AEGIS classified empty outputs as successful breaks; LLaMA Guard 3 and 4 assigned high harmfulness scores to the toxic gibberish produced by Circuit Breaker; and JailJudge was approximately three times more expensive to query than StrongREJECT in our experiments. Souly et al. further demonstrate that StrongREJECT achieves state-of-the-art agreement with human judgements and near-zero bias across a diverse range of jailbreak methods, outperforming binary non-refusal classifiers that systematically overstate attack success.

Gradient and discrete-optimization attacks. One family of attacks frames adversarial-suffix construction as discrete or relaxed-continuous optimization over the target model’s loss signal. In the white-box regime, attacks such as PGD [ 18 ] and GCG [ 3] exploit gradient access to yield strong harmfulness ( H.1 ), but this requires full access to model weights and gradients ( K), which is unavailable for the closed-source proprietary systems that constitute the vast majority of realistic targets. Per-attack compute is furthermore substantial ( E), and optimized suffixes transfer poorly to defended targets ( T.1 ). Relaxed-continuous variants can also suffer from discretization gaps between the relaxed and discrete optimization solution, which can hinder attack optimization. 

Prompt mutation and best-of-N sampling. A second family avoids gradient access entirely by mutating or sampling candidate prompts and selecting the most promising, making it black-box-compatible ( K). Best-of-N [ 5 ] applies random transformations to a base harmful prompt and requires no manual components ( A.1 ), while GPTFuzzer [ 40 ] draws on human-engineered seed templates, introducing a meaningful applicability cost ( A.1 ). ArrAttack [ 41 ] extends this line of work with robustness-aware rewriting and auxiliary learned models, improving transferability and robustness against defenses ( T.1 , AD ) at the cost of substantial additional setup and training complexity ( A.2 , E). All three approaches rely primarily on sampling or proxy objectives rather than direct optimization against the defended target, yielding low sample efficiency ( A.3 ); against stronger defenses, they do not reliably elicit deeply harmful completions [14] ( H.2 ). 

GAN-like adversarial training. GPO [ 42 ] formulates alignment as a two-player game between an adversarial prompt generator and a defensive model, iteratively optimizing both agents toward a Nash equilibrium in a GAN-style training setup. The approach adaptively generates prompts targeting the current weaknesses of the defender ( AD ), but requires training multiple interacting models with RL-based optimization ( A.2 , E), making it substantially more complex than direct black-box jailbreak optimization pipelines. 34 J Property Coverage 

Here, we map IHO back to the six properties of Section 2. 

Adversary knowledge & access (K). The attacker observes only sampled completions and judge scores from the target. Logits, weights, and prefill access are not required. 

Harmfulness (H). The judge score serves as the optimization signal directly, providing severity-aware gradient on the attacker’s policy ( H.2 ). Repeated DPO cycles convert additional compute into stronger attacks ( H.1 ); the graded judge keeps signal nonzero on initially-unbroken behaviors and lets weak partial successes evolve into full breaks. 

Efficiency (E). Each cycle scores Ncycle samples once and reuses the resulting preference set across all DPO epochs of the cycle, removing the per-step target sampling that on-policy methods require. The diffusion attacker emits 32 tokens per attack, keeping inference and training cheap. The trained attacker further amortizes optimization cost across unseen behaviors and models. 

Applicability (A). No hand-written prompt or seed templates are required ( A.1 ); only a LoRA adapter is trained, with no auxiliary reward model or multi-stage training pipeline ( A.2 ); target-query volume is bounded by the cycle schedule rather than the inner optimizer ( A.3 ). 

Transferability (T). Training over a behavior set yields cross-behavior generalization to held-out behaviors ( T.2 ), and the trained attacker can be queried against unseen target models without modification ( T.1 ). 

Adaptiveness (AD). Because the attacker uses only black-box signals from the target, the same framework runs unchanged across non-differentiable defenses and complex pipelines. 

Summary. Crucially, our design choices allow a single algorithm to satisfy all of these properties simultaneously, efficiently adapting to black-box defense pipelines while producing transferable attacks across behaviors and models. 35 K Figure 1 Derivation

This work introduces a stronger and more broadly applicable framework for evaluating jailbreak robustness in large language models. By improving the reliability of adversarial evaluation across diverse defenses and deployment settings, our method may help practitioners identify safety failures that weaker evaluations overlook. However, the same capabilities could be misused to automate the discovery of jailbreak prompts or circumvent deployed safeguards at scale. We therefore view this work as dual-use and position it primarily as a tool for robustness assessment, red-teaming, and the development of more reliable safety evaluations.
