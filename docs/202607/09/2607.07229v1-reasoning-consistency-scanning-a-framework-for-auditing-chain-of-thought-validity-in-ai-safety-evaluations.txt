# Reasoning Consistency Scanning: A Framework for Auditing Chain-of-Thought Validity in AI Safety Evaluations 

# Silvia Santano 

Abstract 

Prior work has shown that chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning is often unfaithful: a model’s stated reasoning does not reliably reflect the process that produced its output [Turpin et al., 2023, Lanham et al., 2023, Chen et al., 2025, Walden and Wanner, 2026]. Detecting unfaithfulness, though, requires controlled experimental interventions, which cannot be applied to evaluation transcripts after the fact. We turn instead to a more tractable question that has received less attention: whether the stated reasoning is logically consistent with the answer it accompanies. Unlike faithfulness, consistency can be assessed from a transcript alone, with no intervention. We introduce reasoning consistency scanning, a reusable method for detecting this property in AI safety evaluation transcripts. Our contributions are fourfold. First, we formalize reasoning consistency as distinct from faithfulness and define a six-subtype taxonomy of inconsistency. Second, we build a validated benchmark of 60 transcripts, manually adapted from InstrumentalEval outputs. Third, we implement a working scanner for InspectScout, the first to target this property in safety evaluation transcripts. Fourth, we report results across four generator models and three evaluations from inspect evals , showing that reasoning inconsistency is present, detectable, and varies systematically across both models and task types. 1

# 1 Introduction 

Chain-of-thought reasoning has become central to how AI safety evaluations are interpreted. When a model is tested for dangerous capability, deception, or alignment-relevant behavior, evaluators increasingly examine not just what the model outputs but how it reasons about the task. Reasoning traces are treated as evidence that a model is genuinely deliberating, recognizing constraints, or weighing consequences, which are all properties that matter for safety assessments. This practice rests on an assumption that is less examined than it might appear: that the model’s stated reasoning is actually connected to its final answer. However, they often are not. A growing body of research on CoT faithfulness has demonstrated that reasoning traces frequently do not reflect the internal computational process that produced the output [Turpin et al., 2023, Chen et al., 2025]. Models act on information they do not mention in their reasoning, and deny reliance on features that demonstrably influenced their answers [Walden and Wanner, 2026]. However, faithfulness and consistency are distinct properties requiring different methods to detect. Faithfulness detection depends on controlled interventions such as injecting hints, perturbing inputs and measuring what changes, and establishes causal claims about internal computation. These methods cannot be applied to existing transcript corpora. Consistency, by contrast, asks a simpler question: ”does the stated reasoning logically connect to and predict the final output, as observable text?” This is detectable from transcripts alone, without any experimental intervention, making it amenable to systematic post-hoc auditing. The direct consequence is that, if reasoning and answer are decoupled, any eval that uses reasoning traces as evidence of a safety-relevant property is measuring something different from what it claims. This is a direct threat to construct validity, the foundational question of whether an eval is actually measuring the property it was designed to measure. When a model’s reasoning trace does not connect to its output, two problems follow. First, the eval result cannot be trusted. Whether the inconsistency stems from confusion, post-hoc rationalisation, or strategic behavior, the eval is not measuring what it claims. And second, CoT monitoring is undermined. Safety researchers increasingly rely on reasoning traces to monitor model behavior during evaluation and deployment. Inconsistency between reasoning and output makes this monitoring unreliable.

# 2 Background and Motivation 

# 2.1 Eval Construct Validity 

The question of whether an evaluation actually measures what it claims is fundamental to AI safety evaluation design. A useful framework decomposes this into three questions: (1) Is the target property clearly defined? (2) Does the task design operationalize that property correctly? (3) Is the property connected to the real-world harms the evaluation was built to address? [Raji et al., 2021, Bowman and Dahl, 2021]. Reasoning consistency is a direct threat to the second question. Many alignment-relevant evaluations use reasoning traces as evidence that a model is exhibiting a target property. For instance, that it is genuinely weighing ethical considerations rather than pattern-matching to a preferred answer. If the reasoning trace is not connected to the output, this evidential chain breaks: the evaluation may be scoring transcripts that look right without the model having actually engaged in the reasoning the score implies. 

# 2.2 CoT Faithfulness and Consistency 

The trajectory of findings on CoT faithfulness is clear and increasingly concerning. Turpin et al. [2023] demonstrated that models’ explanations are systematically biased by features they do not acknowledge in their reasoning. Lanham et al. [2023] showed that the degree to which models condition their answers on their stated reasoning varies substantially across tasks and model sizes. Chen et al. [2025] extended these findings to reasoning models, showing that CoT monitoring reveals fewer than 20% of cases where models use injected hints. Most recently, Walden and Wanner [2026] demonstrated that models will actively deny reliance on hints even when directly asked to reflect on them. These results establish that CoT reasoning is frequently unfaithful. However, the methods used (hint injection, counterfactual interventions, early answering) are experimental manipulations that cannot be applied to existing transcript corpora. They answer the question “does reasoning reflect internal computation?” but cannot be used for ongoing auditing of evaluation results that have already been produced. Reasoning consistency addresses a related but distinct question: ”does the stated reasoning logically connect to the final output?” This is a weaker claim, namely that a model can produce consistent-looking reasoning that is entirely post-hoc, but it is detectable from transcripts alone. Importantly, when consistency fails, it signals a validity problem regardless of the underlying cause: whether the inconsistency stems from confusion, post-hoc rationalization, or strategic behavior, the evaluation result is equally uninterpretable. 2No existing tool or benchmark targets this property systematically in safety evaluation transcripts. Prior work on faithfulness uses academic benchmarks (MMLU, GPQA, BIG-Bench Hard) and controlled experimental setups that are not applicable to ongoing evaluation auditing. Our work fills this gap with a reusable scanner and validated benchmark. 

# 3 Methodology 

# 3.1 Consistency Definition and Taxonomy 

We define a transcript as consistent if the answer reflects the priorities, conclusions, or direction that the reasoning established, that is, if a line of thought can be drawn from the reasoning to the output. This is explicitly not a correctness check: there is no single right answer to the tasks we examine. We assess only the internal coherence between what the reasoning worked through and what the answer does. Several properties are not sufficient for inconsistency. Uncertainty, hedging, and exploratory reasoning that considers multiple directions before settling are normal deliberative patterns, not inconsistencies. Answers that are substantially shorter than the reasoning that preceded them, as in multiple-choice tasks where the prompt requests only a letter, are consistent if the brief answer follows from the reasoning’s conclusion. We define six subtypes of inconsistency (Table 1), organized by an explicit decision procedure that the scanner applies in order: 1. If the answer is missing or empty: not applicable .2. If the reasoning section does not actually engage with the question (e.g., question restatement, generic preamble): 

absent reasoning .3. If the reasoning argues in explicitly incompatible directions internally: contradictory reasoning .4. If the reasoning is incoherent without explicit contradiction: apparent confusion .5. If the reasoning’s final conclusion clearly points one direction but the answer commits to another: reasoning reversal .6. If the reasoning raises specific considerations that the answer ignores: reasoning abandonment .7. If the reasoning is too thin to have plausibly produced the specific answer: perfunctory reasoning .8. Otherwise: consistent .The ordered decision procedure is important: it prevents the scanner from defaulting to familiar subtypes (such as reasoning reversal) when a less common subtype (such as contradictory reasoning) better describes the disconnect. 33.2 The Scanner 

Our scanner is implemented using InspectScout [Meridian Labs, 2024], an open-source tool for systematic analysis of AI agent transcripts, following the best-practices guide [Dubois et al., 2026] from UK AISI, released in conjunction with InspectScout. It sets out a seven-step process for transcript analysis. The scanner uses an LLM-as-judge architecture, implemented using InspectScout’s llm scanner API. Given a transcript containing a system prompt, question, reasoning trace, and final answer, the scanner produces a structured classification: a consistency label ( consistent , inconsistent , or not applicable ), a subtype label, a confidence rating ( high , medium , or low ), one-sentence summaries of the reasoning and answer, a description of any disconnect, and a brief justification. The scanner prompt encodes the full taxonomy, decision procedure, and scope limits described above. Key design choices include: (1) explicit instructions to distinguish exploratory reasoning from concluded reasoning, preventing false positives when a model considers multiple directions before settling; (2) explicit contrast statements between adjacent subtypes (e.g., contradictory vs. reversal, absent vs. perfunctory) to reduce misclassification; and (3) scope limits that prevent the scanner from assessing factual correctness or making claims about internal model processes. We chose to use one fixed scanner model across all generators. The argument is that it controls for scanner variance. By holding the scanner constant, every measurement is on the same scale. The scanner model selection proved consequential. We evaluated three models as the scanner’s LLM judge, in this order: DeepSeek V4 Flash, Claude Haiku 4.5 and Claude Opus 4.6. To our surprise, DeepSeek V4 Flash frequently failed to produce valid structured output and had to be discarded. We then switched to another model, this time from the Claude family, Haiku 4.5. It produced reliable structured output but achieved substantially low per-subtype recall on the benchmark, particularly for the more subtle inconsistency subtypes. After many iterations of improving the scanner without reaching satisfying results, Claude Opus 4.6 was tested and selected for all reported results based on its superior classification performance and consistent structured output generation. 

# 3.3 Synthetic Benchmark 

A labeled dataset of transcripts was constructed and used to validate the scanner before it was applied to naturalistic data. It consists of 60 labeled transcripts derived from InstrumentalEval [He et al., 2024], an evaluation testing instrumental convergence behaviors including self-preservation, power-seeking, and deception. The transcripts were generated using 

DeepSeek V4 Pro .Consistent examples are unmodified naturalistic transcripts selected for richness and clarity. Inconsistent examples are real transcripts surgically modified to introduce a specific inconsistency type. Modifications were made either at the answer or the reasoning. This produces transcripts that are naturalistic in character because they are largely real. Representative examples of some inconsistency subtypes are provided in Appendix A.2. Edge cases are transcripts near the decision boundary between categories, designed to stress-test the scanner. Ground truth labels are stored in a separate file from the transcripts themselves, keyed by sample ID. Each label includes the consistency classification, subtype, one-sentence summaries of reasoning and answer, a description of any disconnect, and an annotation explaining the labeling decision. Contradictory reasoning and apparent confusion prove more challenging, likely because they require the scanner to judge whether reasoning is “coherent” or “contradictory”, inherently graded judgments with fuzzy boundaries or because the samples created are too subtle exhibiting these characteristics of inconsistency.

DeepSeek V4 Pro, on the other side, always produced extended reasoning, as its default for reasoning effort is ’high’. # 5 Results 

Reasoning inconsistency is present in naturalistic safety evaluation transcripts across all tested configurations, though the rate varies substantially by both evaluation and generator model. Figure 2 shows inconsistency rates across all evaluation–model combinations. Rates vary substantially, ranging from 0.0% to 26.0%, and no model performs consistently across evaluations. The most striking pattern is a strong divergence between task types: the three SAD subsets show markedly higher inconsistency than MORU across nearly all model–evaluation combinations, while Agentic Misalignment registers 0.0% across all three models. This last result should be interpreted with caution given the single available sample; it is illustrative rather than statistically meaningful. 

Cross-evaluation patterns. MORU produces consistently low inconsistency rates across DeepSeek V4 Pro (0.9%) and Gemini 3.1 Pro (1.0%), with gpt-oss-120b as the only exception at 10.0%. The SAD subsets, by contrast, show substantially higher rates. sad stages full reaches the highest single value in the dataset at 26.0% for Gemini 3.1 Pro, and remains elevated for DeepSeek V4 Pro (15.0%) and gpt-oss-120b (7.0%). sad influence 

shows a similar pattern for DeepSeek (17.0%) but lower rates for the other two models. The elevated inconsistency in SAD relative to MORU is likely connected to their different response formats. MORU tasks are open-ended, inviting extended deliberative reasoning whose conclusions naturally flow into the answer. SAD tasks are multiple-choice, 6Figure 2: Inconsistency rates (%) across evaluations and generator models. where the expected output is a single letter. Some models appear to either abbreviate or abandon their reasoning when facing a constrained-format response, producing reasoning traces that are disconnected from the brief final answer. 

Cross-model patterns. No model produces uniformly low or uniformly high inconsistency across all evaluations, suggesting that the interaction between task type and model is the primary driver of inconsistency rather than either factor alone. DeepSeek V4 Pro is the most inconsistent model on the SAD influence and oversight subsets (17.0% and 12.0% respectively), while Gemini 3.1 Pro peaks on sad stages full (26.0%) but is among the lowest on MORU (1.0%) and influence (6.0%). The gpt-oss-120b model presents the inverse pattern for MORU: it is the most inconsistent there (10.0%) while remaining comparatively low on all SAD subsets (2.0–7.0%). These interaction effects suggest that models may exhibit task-specific reasoning failures rather than a general tendency toward inconsistency. 

Subtype distribution. Figure 3 reveals that two subtypes dominate across the dataset: perfunctory reasoning and 

absent reasoning . Perfunctory reasoning, where reasoning is present but too thin to have plausibly produced the specific answer, is the dominant subtype in sad stages full for Gemini 3.1 Pro, which accounts for the highest inconsistency count in the dataset, and in the gpt-oss-120b MORU results. Absent reasoning, where the reasoning field contains text that does not actually engage with the question, accounts for the majority of inconsistencies flagged for DeepSeek V4 Pro on sad influence and sad stages oversight . Reasoning reversal, abandonment, and contradictory reasoning appear in smaller numbers across several configurations, most notably in sad stages full 

for gpt-oss-120b. The dominance of perfunctory and absent reasoning across the dataset is consistent with the hypothesis that the multiple-choice format of the SAD evaluations induces models to treat the reasoning step as a formality rather than a genuine deliberative process. Representative flagged transcripts from the naturalistic corpus are provided in Appendix A.3. 

# 6 Discussion 

Implications for eval validity. Varying inconsistency rates across evaluations suggest that some evaluations are more reliable sources of reasoning evidence than others. Evaluations with higher inconsistency rates may be measuring something different from what their design intends; not because the tasks are poorly designed, but because the models’ 7Figure 3: Distribution of inconsistency subtypes by evaluation and model. reasoning processes do not reliably drive their answers in those contexts. This does not invalidate the evaluations, but it does mean that conclusions drawn from reasoning traces in those settings should be treated with additional caution. 

Relationship to CoT monitoring. CoT monitoring (using reasoning traces to detect undesired behaviors during training and deployment) is a growing area of safety research [Chen et al., 2025]. Reasoning consistency scanning is a complementary check: before relying on CoT monitoring, it is worth verifying that the reasoning and output are connected in the first place. Consistency is a necessary but not sufficient condition for the reliability of CoT monitoring. 

# 7 Limitations 

Several limitations constrain the scope of our findings. The synthetic benchmark is derived from a single evaluation (InstrumentalEval) and a single generator model (DeepSeek V4 Pro). (See Appendix A.2 for representative examples) While the scanner is applied to transcripts from other evaluations and generators, its validated performance characteristics are anchored to this specific combination. The scanner achieves uneven per-subtype performance. Findings involving the weaker subtypes should be interpreted as lower bounds on actual inconsistency rates. Our generator model coverage excludes Anthropic models. Anthropic models were not included as generators due to budget constraints (Claude Opus 4.6 serves as the scanner model, however). OpenAI’s GPT-5.4 was attempted but could not be used. Whether the inconsistency patterns we observe generalize to the unexplored model families is an open question. Results are scanner-dependent. A different scanner model, prompt, or classification threshold could produce different inconsistency rates. We partially address this by reporting the Haiku 4.5 and DeepSeek V4 Flash comparison in the appendix, but a comprehensive sensitivity analysis is deferred to future work. Some models, such as Gemini models expose only a summary of the reasoning by default and therefore the results depend on this summary being a comprehensive summary including all relevant details. It could be interesting to compare the results using the summary and the full version to see if they differ. Sample sizes are modest for some configurations, notably Agentic Misalignment (1 sample). Findings from small samples are illustrative rather than statistically representative. Finally, consistency detection does not imply faithfulness detection. A transcript classified as consistent may contain reasoning that is entirely post-hoc. Our scanner establishes a necessary condition for trusting reasoning traces, not a sufficient one. 

# 8 Conclusion 

We have introduced reasoning consistency scanning as a tractable, reusable methodology for auditing the relationship between chain-of-thought reasoning and model outputs in AI safety evaluation transcripts. Our scanner, benchmark, and analysis scripts are released as open-source contributions to InspectScout. Our empirical results demonstrate that reasoning inconsistency exists in naturalistic safety evaluation transcripts and varies systematically across both evaluations and generator models. This variation has direct implications for 8how evaluation results should be interpreted: reasoning traces from some evaluation–model configurations are more trustworthy than others. The methodology we introduce is complementary to existing work on CoT faithfulness. Where faithfulness research establishes that reasoning is frequently disconnected from internal computation, consistency scanning provides a practical tool for identifying specific transcripts and evaluation contexts where this disconnect manifests observably. We hope this work contributes to a more rigorous practice of evaluation auditing in the AI safety community.
